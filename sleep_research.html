Advances in Deep Learning for Automatic Sleep Stage Classification: A Comprehensive Survey

Abstract

Sleep stage classification is a critical component of sleep medicine and neuroscience research. Traditionally performed using polysomnography (PSG) and manual annotation, automatic sleep staging has emerged as a promising field leveraging machine learning and, more recently, deep learning methods. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs), recurrent neural networks (RNNs), attention-based mechanisms, and transformer architectures have significantly advanced performance across various datasets.

This survey reviews recent progress in automatic sleep staging with a focus on model architectures, feature extraction techniques, multimodal integration, and dataset usage. We systematically analyze representative models such as SleePyCo, DeepSleepNet-Lite, SleepEGAN, L-SeqSleepNet, XSleepNet, CTCNet, and others, highlighting their contributions, advantages, and limitations. Furthermore, we provide a quantitative overview of dataset popularity (e.g., Sleep-EDF, SHHS, MASS, ISRUC) and methodological trends. Finally, we discuss open challenges, including class imbalance, generalizability, real-time implementation, and future directions.

1. Introduction

Sleep staging is crucial in healthcare, but manual annotation is time-consuming and subjective. With the rise of deep learning, automatic sleep staging has advanced significantly. This survey provides a structured overview of recent models, datasets, preprocessing strategies, and future challenges in this domain.

2. Background

2.1 Sleep Architecture and Staging Rules

Sleep is divided into Wake, N1, N2, N3, and REM stages based on AASM guidelines. These stages reflect brain activity and physiological signals during rest.

2.2 Data Sources

Polysomnography (PSG) is the gold standard, recording EEG, EOG, EMG, ECG, and respiration. Wearables also provide PPG and limited EEG, expanding accessibility.

3. Datasets

Widely used datasets include Sleep-EDF, SHHS, MASS, ISRUC, MGH, and MESA. Sleep-EDF and SHHS dominate research, while ISRUC and MASS include pathological cases. Dataset diversity influences model generalization.

4. Deep Learning Models

4.1 CNN-based Models

CNNs are dominant for local feature extraction. Examples: DeepSleepNet-Lite, CNN-attention hybrids. They are efficient but struggle with long-term dependencies.

4.2 RNN-based Models

RNNs (e.g., BiLSTM, GRU) model sequential dependencies. Example: L-SeqSleepNet. They capture temporal context but are computationally heavy.

4.3 Transformers and Attention

Transformers are increasingly used, e.g., SleePyCo, CTCNet, SleepTransformer. They provide interpretability and model intra/inter-epoch dependencies but require high memory.

4.4 Hybrid Models

Combining CNN + Transformer (e.g., CTCNet) or GAN-based approaches (e.g., SleepEGAN) improves performance. Graph-based and multimodal methods (EEG+EOG+EMG) are emerging.

4.5 Lightweight and Real-Time Models

DeepSleepNet-Lite and microcontroller-based models enable wearable deployment. These prioritize efficiency and low resource use.

5. Preprocessing & Features

Strategies range from raw EEG input to preprocessing with FFT, CWT, STFT, and ICA. Recent work explores self-supervised pretraining to reduce reliance on heavy feature engineering.

7. Open Challenges & Future Directions

  • Class imbalance (especially N1 detection)
  • Generalization to pathological populations
  • Lightweight models for real-time wearables
  • Improved interpretability for clinical use
  • Self-supervised and federated learning
  • Cross-modal signal integration

8. Conclusion

Deep learning has transformed automatic sleep staging, with CNNs and transformers leading progress. Despite significant advances, challenges remain in generalization, class imbalance, and real-time deployment. Future research will focus on lightweight, interpretable, multimodal, and self-supervised models for broader clinical adoption.
